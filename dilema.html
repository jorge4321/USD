The Innovator's Dilemma

Why great companies fail by doing everything right.

Based on the book by Clayton Christensen

The Core Thesis

Successful companies fail not because of bad management, but because they follow "good" management principles too closely.

Sustaining Technologies

Innovations that improve product performance along dimensions that mainstream customers already value.

Disruptive Technologies

Initially worse performance, cheaper, simpler, smaller. They appeal to a different, niche market.

The Intersection

Disruptive tech improves fast enough to eventually meet the needs of the mainstream market.

Why Incumbents Miss It

Large companies listen to their best customers. Their best customers do NOT want the disruptive technology (yet).

RPV Theory

What a company can and cannot do is defined by its Resources, Processes, and Values.

Resources

Tangible assets: People, equipment, technology, product designs, brands, cash.

Resources are flexible; they can be moved to new problems.

Processes

The patterns of interaction, coordination, communication, and decision making.

Processes are rigid. They are designed to do the same task efficiently, not new tasks.

Values

The criteria by which prioritization decisions are made. "How do we decide what to do?"

The Small Market Problem

"Small markets don't solve the growth needs of large companies."

Upmarket Migration

Companies flee low-margin markets to head towards high-margin markets, leaving a vacuum at the bottom.

Low-End Disruption

Attack the least profitable customers first. The incumbent is happy to let them go.

New-Market Disruption

Creating a new market where none existed. Competing against "non-consumption."

Case Study: Disk Drives

The 5.25" drive disrupted the 8" drive. It was smaller with less capacity, perfect for the new PC market (Toys), useless for Mainframes.

Case Study: Excavators

Cable excavators were powerful. Hydraulics were weak but safe. Hydraulics started digging small residential trenches, then grew up.

Case Study: Steel Mini-Mills

Mini-mills melted scrap (cheap/low quality) to make rebar. Integrated mills ignored rebar. Mini-mills moved up to structural steel.

Solution: Spin-offs

Create an independent organization with its own RPV (Resources, Processes, Values) to tackle the disruption.

Discovery Driven Planning

In disruption, you don't know the market. Plan to learn, not to execute a fixed forecast. Fail fast and cheap.

Conclusion

Disruption is a law of nature. You cannot fight it with the same structures used for sustaining growth. Adapt your structure.
